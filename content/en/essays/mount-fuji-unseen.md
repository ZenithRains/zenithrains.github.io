## Kyoto

### I. Hydrangeas and Hot Springs

**May 27, 2024**

“Anyone going to Japan in late June or early July?” @hiski asked.

“I want to go too.”

One clean stroke cut through every complication.

Early June still carried a trace of springtime coolness, perfect for wandering after dark. *Mayonaka no Door / Stay with Me* and the chamber work *Qianli* made good companions for an evening walk. All kinds of discouraging news had disturbed our expectations; we would have to judge its truth for ourselves.

Hiski and I discovered that June marked the beginning of the rainy season—a “month of water.” Mount Fuji would be hidden by cloud and rain; its snowy crown was not to be seen. Besides, would the snow have melted already? Our excursion to Fuji dissolved like a dream or a bubble, although we never stopped hoping for a glimpse.

Hiski went on to draw up a meticulous itinerary: beginning in Kyoto, passing through Suwa, and ending in Tokyo and Kamakura. One of us would leave from Beijing, the other from Shanghai, and we would meet at Kansai International Airport.

A night of fitful sleep, an early flight, a minor episode—and at last we joined forces. Pink shirt, black crossbody bag, silver suitcase: Hiski came toward me smiling. He had contacted our Airbnb host, Mr. Yoshio, in advance to tell him when we expected to arrive. A sense of cultural otherness surrounded us. This was Kansai Airport.

The Hello Kitty–themed *Haruka* crossed the bridge over the sea. The view beyond the window drifted by at ease. Now and then, someone cycled past. Detached houses formed broad patches, punctuated by apartment towers. Level crossings: many. Density of sports halls: high. I opened Google Maps; the location dot racing across the screen recorded the beginning of our journey.

At around two in the afternoon, after skirting the Osaka Loop Line, the *Haruka* reached JR Kyoto Station. Stepping toward the exit felt like entering another world. The moment we emerged, Kyoto Tower stood before us under blue sky and white clouds. We found the bus stop and took the B2 along Horikawa, past Nishi Hongan-ji and Nijō Castle, until it delivered us to the Nishijin district in northern Kyoto.

Kyoto bus stops are named for the intersection of one east–west road and one north–south road. Hiski handled the conversation with Mr. Yoshio. Our host carefully explained the accommodation and nearby transport. He had even made his own hand-drawn map—plain, practical, and very thoughtful.

Base secured; vehicles acquired. We cycled through Kyoto’s residential lanes. Quiet. The buildings stood in an orderly irregularity—compact, but never oppressive. Along the way were Japanese wooden houses whose colors and materials suggested decades of age, as well as little Western-style homes.

We unexpectedly passed the graves of the loyal retainers of Akō. Political posters appeared everywhere along the road, especially those of the Japanese Communist Party.

Our first stop was Seimei Shrine; our second, the grave of Murasaki Shikibu. More nearby temples followed. All were very simple. Yet what stayed with me most were the hydrangeas: pale and elegant in blue, profound in purple, and finally pure in white.

We cobbled together a meal at a convenience store and raised our cups to a good beginning. The Kyoto sky slowly took on a dreamlike violet. With clothes and towels in hand, we walked to Ōmiya Onsen. First a shower, then the hot bath, then cold water, then the sauna; we repeated the cycle for an hour. Dry off, drink some milk, take it easy.

A summer night. A light breeze. We bathed in the hot spring, took the air in the lanes, and talked our way home.

### II. The Imperial Palace and Nijō Castle

**June 27, 2024**

A sunlit morning. Time to explore.

Beside our guesthouse, temple grounds were invariably laid with flagstones and small pebbles, with lotus plants growing in pots. Walking along the stone path, we would suddenly encounter another hydrangea.

We timed our departure and went straight to the Kyoto Imperial Palace for a guided tour in Chinese. Every bus stop in Kyoto had a display showing when the next bus would arrive. Whenever a bus stopped, it tilted toward the platform to make boarding and alighting easier.

We entered through Seishomon Gate and gathered at the rest area. At ten in the morning, we followed the guide across ground paved with white gravel. She first explained the *hiwadabuki* roofs, made from layers of cypress bark and used on palaces and shrines. Through Jōmeimon Gate, we saw the Shishinden. The palace gardens revealed stillness in miniature scenes. Even after leaving the palace, we lingered in the surrounding grounds to admire the carefully planted pines and cypresses. Back home, I reopened *The Old Capital*. When I reached the words “the thick cypress-bark roof,” the image suddenly became vivid.

Nijō Castle. Hiski knew his heraldry and identified the Tokugawa crests one by one. The Ninomaru Palace was laid out with precision. Along the “nightingale” floor, footsteps sounded like warblers. To reconstruct the historical scene, the exhibition arranged figures of court nobles on different levels: the shogun above, those of lower rank below. One could imagine the authority of the man who held power. The Honmaru Palace was under restoration at the time, so we continued straight to the foundation of the former castle keep.

Nishi Hongan-ji. First, we visited its Karamon, one of Japan’s three great Chinese-style gates. We removed our shoes and walked through the temple halls, incidentally watching a Buddhist service in silence.

From Nijōjō-mae we went to Kawaramachi Oike. Kyoto City Hall stood on one side of the road; on the other: “The enemy is at Honnō-ji.” This was not the site where Oda Nobunaga died, but it was still amusing to visit. West of the temple, Chikuhō Shorō had the air of an old bookshop. My first impression was of the Ming- and Qing-dynasty calligraphy books stacked outside. Looking further into its story, I learned that Yasunari Kawabata and Hideki Yukawa had both spent time there.

> 「川端さんは当時住んでいた下鴨から散歩がてらに来られて」
>
> “Mr. Kawabata would stroll over from Shimogamo, where he lived at the time.”

But that comes later.

We ate taiyaki as we walked and hesitated for a moment at the Kawaramachi–Sanjō intersection, already deep in Kyoto’s commercial center. We crossed the small bridge and the great bridge at Sanjō, then wandered toward Shijō Bridge. We walked along the Kamo River while cyclists passed at intervals. Dinner was at Kyoto Ushimitsu. The Kikusui was gentle, carrying a fragrance of melon; we were unanimous in our approval.

At seven in the evening, before the sky had darkened, a thousand lanterns were already glowing around the dance stage at Yasaka Shrine. From Hanamikoji to Kennin-ji, we passed from converging crowds of tourists into a quiet temple courtyard. Then along Yamato Ōji to Shijō Keihan and home. From the bus, we watched lively Kyoto grow quiet. The two of us mounted our bicycles and headed for the sentō, old hands by now.

When we emerged, the road was silent. Everyone seemed to have fallen asleep.

Nothing remained but Kyoto’s misty night rain.

### III. The Golden Pavilion, Rain, and Sake

**June 28, 2024**

The whisper of rain. I woke on the tatami and slid open the window. A downpour. The world was dim, saturated, alive with water. I drew a deep breath of the damp air and stepped into rain-soaked Kyoto.

We had meant to cycle to Kinkaku-ji under umbrellas. Mr. Yoshio told us that was impossible. Hiski and I had no choice but to catch a bus at Tenjin Kōen-mae. Beneath the shelter, rainwater ran wherever it pleased.

> “*Ame*,” said Hiski.
>
> “*Ame*,” I replied.

The bus was a ship. It rounded the grave of Murasaki Shikibu and made port at Kitaōji. We got off and walked along Kagamiishi Road. On the approach to Rokuon-ji, fine stones crunched beneath our feet. Moss spread over the grounds in broad sheets. My first thought was simply that it was extraordinarily well maintained, though we had already seen much the same at Nijō Castle.

Then the Golden Pavilion burst into view and drew us toward Kyōko-chi, the Mirror Pond. On a clear day, perhaps drifting clouds would cast their leisurely shadows while the stars wheeled through the changing years. But here, behind an endless curtain of rain, ripples unfolded layer upon layer. Evergreen pines stood along the shore. Lichen-clad rocks rose from the water. Distant mountains wore veils woven from mist, while pines and cedars descended their slopes in tiers of pale yellow and deep green. The whole landscape seemed afloat. And then, amid that solitary desolation, there stood between heaven and earth—unmoving, unchanging, radiant—the Golden Pavilion.

> “*Zekkei!*” Hiski.
>
> “*Zekkei!*” Me.

We stared for a long time before moving on.

It had burned once already. Perhaps a shogun had once withdrawn here and gazed over the Mirror Pond. Perhaps—perhaps, as *The Temple of the Golden Pavilion* says—“I want to live.” History and literature intertwined. A small waterfall hid in the dense woods. We admired the Land Boat Pine, every needle studded with raindrops.

At Kitano Tenmangū, students on school trips filled the grounds with noise. The great *chinowa* ring had been erected for the summer purification ceremony on June 30. Ignoring the downpour, we threw aside our umbrellas and passed through it: two bows, two claps, one final bow. Hiski casually drew a fortune. Number one. Great blessing.

We had lunch at Sora wa Kimagure. Pork cutlet with hamburger steak: the essence of a set meal. Hiski ordered a Dr Pepper; I chose orange juice. Caramel pudding followed.

Kawaramachi Imadegawa. This time, the Kamo River in rain. Two or three ducks rested along the bank. We crossed Demachi Bridge, followed the approach into Tadasu no Mori, detoured to Kawai Shrine, and finally reached Shimogamo Shrine. In a playful mood, we drew water fortunes. Number eleven. Great blessing.

Leaving the quiet desolation of Tadasu no Mori, we set our sights on Kyoto University. We wandered around, climbed Yoshida Hill, and saw Yoshida Dormitory below. Its students had decorated it with complete freedom, and the noticeboards at its entrance left a strong impression. Among them, the line from the twenty-fifth year of Duke Xiang in the *Zuo Zhuan*—“Cui Zhu murdered his lord”—had a resolute force. Kyoto University’s clubs were equally visible on those boards: a Classical Chinese Poetry Society; a Yuri Research Society; a joint declaration opposing higher tuition. We could stand there and laugh aloud. One could not help admiring their will to struggle—the surviving spirit of Zenkyōtō.

Dinner at the cafeteria. Afterward we walked and discussed why Kyoto University had produced so many Nobel laureates. On the way back, our bicycles carried us straight to Funaoka Onsen. After the bath, an izakaya. Hiski and I sat facing each other.

> “*Kanpai!*” Hiski.
>
> “*Kanpai!*” Me.

Laughter and cheerful voices. The sake was exceptionally sweet, and we kept sipping.

Yakumo Shokudō. The background music happened to switch to *Love Story wa Totsuzen ni*.

## Suwa: Around the Lake, the Shrines, Tateishi Park, and Katakurakan

### June 30, 2024

The sky was overcast. Moisture swirled around the observation deck; lead-gray clouds seemed close enough to touch. Rain blurred the glass. The red and white of Kyoto Tower formed a dialogue across time with the *karahafu* gable of Nishi Hongan-ji. We wandered through Kyoto Station in search of food, only to fall back on a street-side café to console the undeniable reality of our hunger.

The Tōkaidō Shinkansen ground Kyoto into fragments along the horizon. Fine rain floated beyond the window. Inside, office workers held their bento boxes like motionless statues.

At Nagoya, we transferred to the JR Chūō Main Line. Hiski brought out *Retrospective 53 Minutes*, beginning with “Hiroshige No. 36.” ZUN’s psychedelic synthesizers merged with the sound of the rails. The track began to climb through the mountains of Shinshū. Moisture clung to the fields. Electrical boxes, utility poles, and level crossings flew backward. The long mountain ranges supplied an outline to a darkened ukiyo-e. Over rushing valleys, through dim tunnels, around sharp bends: this was not merely a train advancing, but an expedition toward Gensokyo.

Steam rose from the footbath at Kami-Suwa Station. The moment we reached Hotel Inn in Kamisuwa, the rain became torrential. At seven, we ventured eight hundred meters through the downpour in search of okonomiyaki. When we finished our mixture of starch and vegetables and opened the door, we fell into a horror game. Black rainy night. Empty streets. Blood-red lights. Silent Western houses. Eerie spires. The only NPC: a convenience-store clerk. We silently bought white raincoats. After all, we planned to cycle around the lake the next day.

### July 1, 2024

Relentless rain. From the tower of Takashima Castle, predictably, Mount Fuji was nowhere to be seen. The lakeside park had been reclaimed from the water; its spread of gravel still betrayed the work. Electric bicycles could not be rented while it rained, so we sheltered at the pier for forty minutes. A sightseeing boat shaped like a swan came and went. Hiski launched a basic attack: *Swan* and *Suwa* were a pun. As soon as the rain eased, we rented two e-bikes, put on our helmets, adjusted the gears, and set out.

Side quest (main quest) activated: Suwa Taisha, the *ichinomiya* of Shinano Province. The torque advantage of an e-bike became obvious on the climb; we reached the top with ease. Four enormous *onbashira* pillars stood around the shrine. On the racks of *ema*, wishes for safety were rivaled only by an overwhelming concentration of Moriya Suwako. The Lower Shrine’s Harumiya and Akimiya not only shared the same blueprint, but had once served as showcases for two competing schools of Suwa craftsmen. Beside Harumiya, rapids surged past Ukishima Shrine.

We plunged downhill toward the lake. The rain paused. Back on the lakeside cycling path, an open-air footbath appeared: “SAVE.” We soaked our feet in the breeze, then continued at leisure. Three quarters of the way around, rain came crashing down. The lake’s surface broke into frantic ripples. Boats along the shore rocked from side to side. We pulled on our raincoats and charged ahead, one behind the other. Rain struck our faces. Exhilarating.

We completed the circuit at three in the afternoon. Public transport here used minivans instead of full-sized buses, perhaps reflecting the combined pressures of local finances, an aging population, and a falling birth rate. At Tateishi Park, we looked down over Lake Suwa. Leaning against the railing, with no inexplicable background music playing, we faced the mirror set into the Central Alps and could only repeat one phrase: “*Zekkei.*”

Katakurakan. A cultural property worthy of the name: Western-style architecture, relief carvings, a great communal bath. Our hot-spring experience had reached a perfect conclusion.

## Tokyo

### I. Labyrinth, Shibuya, Kabukichō

**July 2, 2024**

Summer. Clear skies. The Chūō Special Rapid took two hours and twenty minutes. Sunlight warmed the little woods beyond the window. I opened Google Maps and watched us move. We would pass through Kōfu; perhaps we might see Mount Fuji? Still *dame*.

The train delivered us to Shinjuku Station. “Shinjuku has more than two hundred exits,” Hiski said. I followed him through a multiply connected labyrinth made from exits. Outside Akasaka-mitsuke Station, crowds streamed past as we dragged our luggage up another steep slope. We left our bags at the hotel and found lunch.

At the subway station, we bought two day passes. Shibuya Crossing delivered the stereotypical bombardment of advertising. Tourists in the second-floor Starbucks photographed the flow of people below. We browsed a bookshop, tried the capsule-toy machines, and surveyed Starbucks prices to report back to Iam. We wandered without buying, conscientiously resisting the trap of consumerism. Still thirsty, I let a vending machine spit out a bottle of Dr Pepper. One sip—hmm? It tasted like the long-lost foreign brother of Chinese loquat cough syrup.

Passing the Shibuya City Office, we saw the Tokyo gubernatorial election boards: cats, dogs, an AI robot, the NHK Party, etc. A little too abstract 😅. We crossed Yoyogi Park on the way to Meiji Shrine. The planted forest was quiet. Rows of sake barrels formed a wall inscribed with brewery names. Western tourists arrived in groups.

The Yamanote Line links Ueno, Akihabara, Shinagawa, Shibuya, Shinjuku, Ikebukuro, and several other subcenters and commercial districts. We boarded at Yoyogi and spent an hour making a circuit of Tokyo. I stood deliberately in the first carriage. Through the glass partition between the cab and the passengers, I could watch the driver conduct each point-and-call check with immaculate care. As we passed Ueno, Hiski said, “Lu Xun—the cherry blossoms at Ueno…” It took me a moment to catch the reference.

Back at Yoyogi, we walked to Shinjuku. Above ground, towers crowded the skyline. At 6:15 p.m. Tokyo time, the light was on the verge of fading. We roamed Shinjuku Station’s enormous underground shopping streets, then resurfaced on a pedestrian bridge. Traffic streamed beneath us and lights shone on every side. The city’s energy and complexity demanded admiration.

Night. Past an enormous pachinko parlor lay the entrance to Kabukichō Ichibangai. People stood along both sides of the road, some in vertiginous heels. Staff outside the “free information centers” solicited passersby.

“Sir, do you need any services?” one of them asked in Mandarin. We jumped and hurried away. Looking back, both of us found it strange: how had he identified us as tourists? Some Kabukichō storefronts openly displayed WeChat QR codes, clearly a “business” aimed at ( ). Should we take the opportunity to visit Ōkubo Park? Better not to stand beneath a dangerous wall.

A line of black taxis occupied the road. Every so often, a motorcycle roared past. Yet just across the street stood a Kinokuniya. Kōdansha’s paperback series was interesting: most authors were researchers in the field they wrote about. Even after converting at the favorable exchange rate of the time, the books remained expensive. There were cabinet after cabinet of light novels too, including one particular series being pushed hard alongside its current anime adaptation. To turn from a noisy red-light district into a quiet bookshop: a remarkable contrast.

### II. It’s MyGO!!, Bocchi the Rock!, and Happy Birthday

**July 3, 2024**

At Akasaka Station, magic had invaded the morning rush. Office workers hurried past without listening to portraits whisper. Alchemical symbols could not turn stone into gold, but they had been painted across the ceiling.

Ikebukuro, northwest exit. “I miss you terribly in Ikebukuro”—cheap sentimentality. “The staff like sleeping in; coming early is pointless”—at least that was honest. An ATM hummed and returned my card. Rejected. Try again. Rejected again. Hiski’s VISA card produced cash without friction, while the ICBC UnionPay card in my hand became a slab of useless plastic. At an overseas ICBC branch in Ikebukuro, the teller spoke standard, fluent Mandarin, but even she could not cure an incompatibility between chip standards. In short, the system classified me as “unreadable.” At the east-exit smoking area, men and women in suits legally inhaled and exhaled their anxiety.

Sunshine City. Animate. “Otaku money is so easy to earn.” Character standees, badges, illustration boards. Apart from the *Genshin Impact* artbook, the anime merchandise here was cheaper than at home. I bought an *Uma Musume* acrylic stand and completed ziyang’s shopping list. A breeze crossed the rooftop garden where Sakiko and Uika had met; when we turned around, sunlight was adding still more heat to the air-conditioning exhaust. The bridge where someone had “become human” was under repair. Railway workers mended the road while fans inside their air-conditioned jackets made wind.

Shimokitazawa. Vintage clothing shops copied and pasted down the street. The live house walls displayed gig announcements and “No Photography” signs. By day, sunlight; by night, little Bocchi—but Freedom. There was no real Hitori Gotoh at the sacred site.

Time for data to overwrite the artistic filter. Yesterday, a Shibuya MUJI had advertised an hourly wage of ¥1,400. At a Shimokitazawa supermarket: a Black Beauty watermelon, ¥1,511; a bag of apples, ¥626; one melon, ¥3,239; four loquats, ¥734. The cost of living in Tokyo was concrete—concrete down to the weight of four loquats.

Leaving Shimokitazawa, we passed a secondhand record shop, where the vinyl occasionally bore scratches. Outside an old bookshop, Hiski encountered the *Shinchō Kōki*. One hundred yen. The whole life of Oda Nobunaga had deflated to one fourteenth of an hourly wage.

The slope of Sakurazaka climbed toward Toranomon. Here had stood the old Hotel Okura, the headquarters of the Black Dragon Society, and the birthplace of the Chinese United League. A monument marked the site. The setting sun spilled through the leaves like gold dust, falling with perfect equality on foreign embassies and the headquarters of various Japanese cults.

Azabudai Hills. This had been our budget substitute for a night view, the product of careful calculation. A staff member said:

> The café is booked for a private party.

Perhaps that, too, was a cost of living. Our value-for-money plan collapsed. Then to the Tower!

Red, orange-red, warm, steadfast. We ascended to the Main Deck. A gigantic integrated circuit board spread beneath our feet, its gold-red streams of light pulsing without rest. The halo of Shinjuku. The curve of Odaiba. Warm yellow points in apartment windows. At a height of 150 meters, the city’s clamor had been filtered into a low murmur.

In that flowing, phantasmagoric city, somewhere between losing the way and searching, another *maigo* grew one year older.

### III. Simulated Annealing and Low-Frequency Noise

The public-account version 1.0 was too linear, torsion-free, flatly chronological. Time for a little simulated annealing—to perturb the prose.

**4:00 p.m., July 4, 2024**

We drifted through pages like emoticons. Just as we were about to leave Jinbōchō, a book on a stall caught our eyes. Slanting out from a heap of Japanese physics notes was a red-covered copy of Stein’s *Topics in Harmonic Analysis Related to the Littlewood–Paley Theory*.

The stacks of Jinbōchō were as convoluted as that title.

Of course I did not buy it. But later it suddenly reminded me that perhaps this day should not be written in chronological order at all. It should be written as a spectrum. Jump from the time domain into the frequency domain; perform a high–low-frequency decomposition. Clamor becomes high-frequency oscillation. Restlessness becomes low-frequency background noise. The standard depends entirely on a weight function of my own design.

Yes. Faulkner be praised.

#### High Frequency ^^^^^^^

From the white noise of an *Arknights: Here a People Sows* poster and *Zenless Zone Zero* advertisements, we squeezed into the cramped aisles of Don Quijote. With the exchange rate at a historic low, the Shibuya Apple Store offered an arbitrage window of size ε. From there, we made another detour to Akihabara’s secondhand electronics streets.

We explored the little green building from basement level one to the sixth floor, purely for amusement. The all-pervading assault on sight, touch, and hearing instead produced immediate, complete desensitization. Subjects people would ordinarily be embarrassed to discuss had all been converted into products payable by QR code. We complained that it was *Commodified, All Too Commodified*, and then broke into laughter anyway.

I had never seen commercial display so honest.

#### Middle Frequency ~~~~~~~

Several trains were about to cross beneath Hijiri Bridge: a storyboard from *Suzume*. Such an everyday scene gained meaning only at the instant we observed it. It was entirely a probabilistic event.

Holding a folder aloft, a guide explained Orthodox saints among the flickering candles of Holy Resurrection Cathedral. At Sensō-ji, the clamor itself was the incense.

We split up. Hiski went off to acquire precise railway atlases. A group of middle-school students from Shanghai entered Uchiyama Bookstore and displaced me to the third floor, where Chinese books were stored in distributed fashion. At Shangjia Bookstore, before lingering over works by Shirō Matsumoto and Moriichi Yamazaki, I pulled a book on distribution theory from the mathematics shelf.

#### Singularity •

Marunouchi looked like a castle, although, as Hiski observed, an explanation in terms of a ship worked just as well. The Imperial Palace was a singular point in the land of Tokyo. In the slanting western sun, red-brick Tokyo Station and the high-rises still under construction stood less than a century apart.

Speaking of which, where did the low-frequency section go?

—After a full day of walking, we were low-frequency enough. We were tourists.
